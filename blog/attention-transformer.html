Attention & the Transformer:
The Architecture That Changed Everything

In 2017, eight Google engineers wrote a paper called "Attention Is All You Need" — and nothing in AI has been the same since. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA, AlphaFold, Stable Diffusion — all built on this one idea. The central insight: instead of processing words one by one and hoping the model remembers what came before, let every word look at every other word simultaneously and decide for itself what is relevant. This guide explains the transformer from absolute zero — why Q, K, and V exist, what each one truly means, why Key and Value are two separate things, and how the whole mechanism works as a beautifully consistent system.

"The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired."
What does "it" refer to — the animal, or the street?
You knew instantly. You attended to the right word.

For a decade, building a machine that could do the same

01 — The Central Idea: Every Word Talks to Every Other Word

Before transformers, language models processed text like reading a book through a keyhole — one word at a time, left to right. To connect "it" to "animal" in a 50-word sentence, information had to travel through 48 hidden state updates, each one potentially overwriting what came before. The further apart two words were, the harder it was to connect them.

The transformer throws this constraint away entirely. Its core idea is strikingly simple: let every word look at every other word at the same time, and let the model decide how much each pair is related. Not "word 1 then word 2 then word 3" — all words simultaneously, all relationships computed in parallel.

🏛️ Non-Tech Analogy: The Town Hall Meeting

Imagine a sentence as a group of people in a room. In an old RNN model, they pass a message around a circle — person 1 whispers to person 2, who whispers to person 3. By the time the message reaches person 20, it is barely recognisable.

In a transformer, everyone in the room speaks at once. Every person can hear every other person. Each person decides how much attention to pay to each voice — based on what they personally need to figure out right now. "It" listens carefully for a noun it could refer to. "Animal" raises its voice in response. "Street" stays quiet. The word "it" updates its understanding based on what it heard — mostly "animal." This is attention.

02 — The Core Question: How Does a Word Decide What to Pay Attention To?

Here is the problem the architects had to solve. You have a sentence: "The cat sat on the mat because it was comfortable." The word "it" needs to figure out what it refers to. It can look at every other word — but how does it score them? How does it decide "mat" is more relevant than "sat" here?

The answer the paper proposed is elegant: split each word's representation into three different roles, and use those roles to compute relationships. Every word simultaneously acts as:

  1. Something that is looking for relevant information from others
  2. Something that announces what it can offer to those looking
  3. Something that delivers the actual information once it is selected

These three roles are named Query, Key, and Value — borrowed from the world of databases and information retrieval. Understanding what each one truly means is the key to understanding the entire transformer.

03 — Q, K, V: What They Are and Why They're Named That Way

Q
Query — "What am I looking for?"
The question a word broadcasts to the whole sequence. "It" produces a Query that effectively asks: "Which word in this sentence am I a pronoun for?" Every other word receives this question and must decide whether it has a relevant answer.
K
Key — "What can I offer? How should I be found?"
The label a word puts on itself so others can find it. "Cat" and "mat" both produce Keys describing their nature. The Key is not the content itself — it is a searchable description of what the word has to offer. It controls whether this word gets selected.
V
Value — "What is my actual content?"
The actual information delivered once a word is selected. If "mat" is deemed relevant to "it," the Value of "mat" is what "it" actually absorbs. The Value is the payload — what gets passed along after the matching is done.
🔍 Non-Tech Analogy: The Library Database

You search for books about "renewable energy." You type your search term into the system — that is your Query. Every book has a spine label: title, keywords, subject — that is its Key. The system compares your Query against every Key and scores how well each book matches.

Now — the crucial part. The book you pull from the shelf does not contain the keywords on its spine. It contains the actual text — that is the Value. The Key was just the index label used for matching. The Value is what you actually read.

In attention: Query = your search. Key = each word's index label. Value = what each word actually contributes once selected. You match Query against Keys to decide relevance. Then you collect the Values of the most relevant words.

Why Are Key and Value Two Separate Things?

This is the question most explanations skip — and it is the most important one. Why not just match directly on the word embedding, without the Key/Value separation?

The answer is flexibility and context-sensitivity. A word needs to advertise itself differently depending on the relationship type being computed, and deliver different content depending on who is asking. The Key and Value are both learned projections of the same word — but through different weight matrices, which means the model learns to separate "how I want to be found" from "what I give when found."

💡 The K/V Separation — A Concrete Example: "bank"

1. "She sat on the bank of the river."
2. "He deposited money at the bank."

In sentence 1, when "river" queries for a related geographic feature, "bank" should respond with a Key that says "I am a natural location feature." In sentence 2, when "deposited" queries for a financial entity, "bank" should respond with a Key that says "I am a financial institution."

The Values are equally different — what "bank" contributes to nearby words is completely different in each sentence. The Key controls whether "bank" gets selected; the Value controls what "bank" contributes once selected. Separating them lets the model learn both independently through W_K and W_V — two matrices with different roles, trained on the same word representation.

This is why the same word in different contexts produces dramatically different final representations. The Key advertises contextually; the Value delivers contextually. Both are powered by the same input embedding, but each is shaped by its own learned transformation.

The Three Projections — The Learned Matrices

Every word in the sequence produces all three vectors through three separate learned linear transformations (W_Q, W_K, W_V) — weight matrices trained during pre-training:

Q = X · W_Q    ← "What am I looking for?"     W_Q ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_k)
K = X · W_K    ← "What can I offer / how to find me?"   W_K ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_k)
V = X · W_V    ← "What is my actual content?"     W_V ∈ ℝ^(d_model × d_v)

X = token embeddings, shape (seq_len × d_model)
Every word simultaneously produces Q, K, and V for itself.
This is "self-attention" — every word attends to every other word, including itself.
W_Q, W_K, W_V are learned parameters — the model figures out what questions to ask,
how to advertise, and what to deliver, entirely from training data.

04 — The Full Attention Computation: Step by Step

Now that Q, K, and V make intuitive sense, let us trace the full computation. Four steps, each with a clear purpose.

Step 1 — Raw Scores: How Well Does Each Query Match Each Key?

Dot product every token's Query against every other token's Key. The higher the dot product, the more aligned the two vectors are — the more relevant that word is to the query.

Scores = Q · Kᵀ

Result: an (n × n) matrix — entry [i, j] = how much word i wants to attend to word j
n = sequence length. All pairs computed simultaneously.

Step 2 — Scale: Prevent Softmax Saturation

Scaled Scores = Q · Kᵀ / √d_k

Two random d_k-dimensional vectors have a dot product with std dev √d_k
For d_k=64: σ≈8. For d_k=512: σ≈22. Unscaled scores are too large.
Large scores → softmax assigns ≈1 to the max, ≈0 to everything else
→ gradient of softmax ≈ 0 everywhere → training stalls
Dividing by √d_k keeps scores at ≈unit variance regardless of dimension size.

Step 3 — Softmax: Convert Scores to Attention Weights

Weights = softmax( Q · Kᵀ / √d_k )

Each row sums to 1.0 — a probability distribution over the sequence
"it" might produce weights: [0.03, 0.07, 0.03, 0.02, 0.55, 0.01, 1.00, 0.02, 0.27]
                                 The cat sat on mat bec it was comf
→ "mat" dominates. "it" will absorb mostly mat's content.

Step 4 — Weighted Sum of Values: Collect the Content

Output = Weights · V

Each word's new representation = weighted blend of ALL words' Value vectors
"it" absorbs 55% of "mat"'s Value, 27% of "comfortable"'s Value, etc.
The word "it" now carries context from the words it attended to — in its new vector.

All four steps in one formula — the heart of every transformer ever built:

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( Q · Kᵀ / √d_k ) · V

GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, LLaMA-3 — all of them are, at their core, this formula repeated billions of times.

Q = [[1.0, 0.5], ← Cat's query: "What am I looking for?" [0.8, 1.2], ← sat's query [0.3, 0.9]] ← mat's query K = [[1.0, 0.2], ← Cat's key: "What can I offer?" [0.5, 1.0], ← sat's key [0.2, 0.8]] ← mat's key V = [[2.0, 0.0], ← Cat's value: "My actual content" [0.0, 2.0], ← sat's value [1.0, 1.0]] ← mat's value

Step 1: Raw scores for "Cat" (row 0 of Q · Kᵀ)

Cat→Cat: (1.0×1.0)+(0.5×0.2) = 1.10 ← Cat querying itself
Cat→sat: (1.0×0.5)+(0.5×1.0) = 1.00 ← Cat querying "sat"
Cat→mat: (1.0×0.2)+(0.5×0.8) = 0.60 ← Cat querying "mat"

Step 2: Scale by 1/√2 ≈ 0.707

[1.10, 1.00, 0.60] × 0.707 = [0.778, 0.707, 0.424]

Step 3: Softmax → attention weights

exp([0.778, 0.707, 0.424]) = [2.177, 2.028, 1.528]  sum = 5.733
weights = [0.380, 0.354, 0.266]

Step 4: Weighted sum of Values

0.380 × [2.0, 0.0] = [0.760, 0.000] ← from Cat's Value
0.354 × [0.0, 2.0] = [0.000, 0.708] ← from sat's Value
0.266 × [1.0, 1.0] = [0.266, 0.266] ← from mat's Value

"Cat" new representation = [1.026, 0.974]

🔍 What Just Happened

"Cat" started as its own isolated embedding. After one attention operation, its representation is a contextual blend: 38% of its own content, 35.4% of "sat"'s content (it did something), 26.6% of "mat"'s content (there was a location). The word "Cat" now encodes relationships — its representation is context-aware.

Scale to 768 dimensions, 12 heads, 12 layers, 30,000 vocabulary items: BERT. Scale to 12,288 dimensions, 96 heads, 96 layers: GPT-3. The operation is identical. Only the size changes.

06 — Multi-Head Attention: Attending to Many Relationships at Once

Single-head attention produces one score per token pair — one relationship type. But language has many simultaneous relationship types: syntactic (subject-verb agreement), semantic (synonymy), coreference (pronoun resolution), positional (nearby context). A single head must average over all of them. It is like trying to answer multiple questions from a group at once — you miss the nuance.

Multi-head attention runs H independent attention operations in parallel, each with its own W_Q, W_K, W_V. Each head learns to specialise in a different type of relationship.

👂 Analogy: A Panel of Specialists Reading the Same Text

Imagine giving a complex legal document to a panel: one logician (tracks if-then structure), one entity specialist (tracks who owes what to whom), one timeline expert (tracks before/after), one terminology specialist (tracks legal terms). All reading the same document simultaneously, each noting different things.

Each attention head is a specialist. Head 1 might learn to track subject-verb agreement. Head 2 learns coreference. Head 3 learns local context. Head 4 learns semantic similarity. They all process the same input — they just attend to different aspects of it.

For each head h = 1 … H:
  head_h = Attention(X·W_Q^h,  X·W_K^h,  X·W_V^h)
  d_k = d_model / H  (each head works in a smaller subspace)

MultiHead(X) = Concat(head_1, …, head_H) · W_O

Concatenate all heads → shape (seq, d_model). W_O learns to mix information from all heads.
BERT-base: H=12, d_model=768, d_k=64 per head
GPT-3:    H=96, d_model=12288, d_k=128 per head

07 — Positional Encodings: Adding the Sense of Order

Attention is position-blind. The dot product Q·Kᵀ measures semantic similarity — no notion of which word came first. "Cat sat mat" and "Mat sat cat" look identical to attention alone. Positional encodings add a position-dependent signal to each word's embedding before attention runs, breaking this symmetry.

08 — Causal Masking: How GPT Stays Honest During Training

Standard attention lets every word see every other word — including future ones. For a model learning to predict the next word, seeing future words is cheating: you can just copy the answer. Decoder models (GPT, LLaMA, Claude) apply a causal mask: add −∞ to future positions before softmax. Since exp(−∞) = 0, future tokens get exactly zero weight.

Causal mask for seq_len=4:

      tok1   tok2   tok3   tok4
tok1 [ 0.0, -inf, -inf, -inf] ← sees only itself
tok2 [ 0.0,  0.0, -inf, -inf] ← sees tok1 and itself
tok3 [ 0.0,  0.0,  0.0, -inf] ← sees tok1, tok2, itself
tok4 [ 0.0,  0.0,  0.0,  0.0] ← sees all previous

At inference: the model reads "The capital of France is" and predicts "Paris." It appends "Paris" and predicts the next token. One token at a time, left to right — exactly matching how it was trained.

09 — The Complete Transformer Block

A transformer stacks identical blocks. Each block has two sub-layers, each wrapped with a residual connection and layer normalisation:

Sub-layer 1: Multi-Head Self-Attention
   x = x + MultiHeadAttention( LayerNorm(x) )

Sub-layer 2: Feed-Forward Network
   x = x + FFN( LayerNorm(x) )

FFN(x) = GELU( x · W₁ + b₁ ) · W₂ + b₂
W₁: d_model → 4×d_model (expand). W₂: 4×d_model → d_model (contract).
Attention sub-layer
Mixes information across positions. "It" absorbs context from "animal." Relationships between any two words — no distance penalty.
Feed-Forward Network
Transforms information within each position. Attention mixes; FFN processes. Research finds factual knowledge ("Paris is capital of France") encoded in FFN activations, not attention weights.
Residual connection (x + …)
Creates a direct gradient highway — backpropagation flows through the + operator without passing through nonlinearities. What makes stacking 12–96 layers stable.
Layer Normalisation
Normalises activations across features after each sub-layer. Prevents activation explosion/collapse as depth increases. Applied before each sub-layer in modern models (Pre-LN).

10 — Code: Attention and Full Transformer Block from Scratch

import torch, math
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# 1. Scaled Dot-Product Attention
#    Q: what I'm looking for  |  K: what I can offer  |  V: what I deliver
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
def scaled_dot_product_attention(Q, K, V, mask=None):
    d_k = Q.size(-1)
    scores = (Q @ K.transpose(-2, -1)) / math.sqrt(d_k)   # (B, H, T, T)
    if mask is not None:
        scores = scores + mask
    weights = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)
    return weights @ V, weights


# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# 2. Multi-Head Attention — H heads, each specialising in different relationships
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
class MultiHeadAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, n_heads):
        super().__init__()
        assert d_model % n_heads == 0
        self.d_k     = d_model // n_heads
        self.n_heads = n_heads
        self.W_q = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)  # what am I looking for?
        self.W_k = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)  # what can I offer?
        self.W_v = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)  # what do I deliver?
        self.W_o = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)  # mix all heads' outputs

    def forward(self, x, mask=None):
        B, T, C = x.shape
        def split(t):
            return t.view(B, T, self.n_heads, self.d_k).transpose(1, 2)
        Q, K, V = split(self.W_q(x)), split(self.W_k(x)), split(self.W_v(x))
        out, attn = scaled_dot_product_attention(Q, K, V, mask)
        out = out.transpose(1, 2).contiguous().view(B, T, C)
        return self.W_o(out), attn


# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# 3. Feed-Forward Network (position-wise, 4× expansion, GELU)
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
class FeedForward(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_model * 4),
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Linear(d_model * 4, d_model),
        )
    def forward(self, x): return self.net(x)


# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# 4. Transformer Block (Pre-LN variant — more stable than original Post-LN)
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
class TransformerBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, n_heads, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.ln1  = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.attn = MultiHeadAttention(d_model, n_heads)
        self.ln2  = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.ffn  = FeedForward(d_model)
        self.drop = nn.Dropout(dropout)

    def forward(self, x, mask=None):
        attn_out, w = self.attn(self.ln1(x), mask)
        x = x + self.drop(attn_out)           # residual
        x = x + self.drop(self.ffn(self.ln2(x)))
        return x, w


# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# 5. Sinusoidal Positional Encoding
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
class SinusoidalPE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, max_len=4096):
        super().__init__()
        pe  = torch.zeros(max_len, d_model)
        pos = torch.arange(0, max_len).unsqueeze(1).float()
        div = torch.exp(torch.arange(0, d_model, 2).float() * -(math.log(10000.0) / d_model))
        pe[:, 0::2] = torch.sin(pos * div)
        pe[:, 1::2] = torch.cos(pos * div)
        self.register_buffer('pe', pe.unsqueeze(0))
    def forward(self, x): return x + self.pe[:, :x.size(1)]


# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# Sanity check
# ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
d_model, n_heads, seq_len = 128, 4, 16
pe    = SinusoidalPE(d_model)
block = TransformerBlock(d_model, n_heads)
x     = pe(torch.randn(2, seq_len, d_model))
mask  = torch.triu(torch.full((seq_len, seq_len), float('-inf')), diagonal=1)
out, attn_w = block(x, mask=mask)
print(f"Input  shape:       {x.shape}")
print(f"Output shape:       {out.shape}")
print(f"Attn weight shape:  {attn_w.shape}")
print(f"Params in block:    {sum(p.numel() for p in block.parameters()):,}")
print(f"Causal mask OK:     {(attn_w[0, 0, 0, 1:] == 0).all().item()}")
print(f"Attn weights sum:   {attn_w[0, 0, 0].sum().item():.4f}")

11 — KV-Cache: Why Chat Generation Is Fast

During text generation, the model produces one token at a time. Without optimisation, it would recompute K and V for every token in context at every step. The KV-cache stores K and V once computed and reuses them. Only the new token's K and V need to be computed each step.

This reduces generation complexity from O(n²) (recompute everything) to O(n) (compute one row, reuse all others). For a 128k-context conversation, the difference is seconds vs minutes per response.

Why cache K and V, but not Q? Because K and V represent fixed properties of each context token — "what I can offer" and "what I deliver" don't change once a token is in the sequence. Q represents what the current generation step is looking for — it changes at every step as the model produces the next token.

13 — A Thought to Sit With

💬 For You to Consider

The transformer was motivated by one linguistic problem: resolving references across long distances. It worked — and then it turned out to work equally well on images, protein structures, audio spectrograms, and video. Domains with completely different structures, governed by completely different physics, all amenable to the same scaled dot product.

What does this universality reveal? Is attention capturing something fundamental about the structure of information — that meaningful patterns in any domain can be expressed as weighted relationships between parts? Or did eight engineers get extraordinarily lucky with a formulation general enough to fit whatever gradient descent was minimising — and we've been reverse-engineering a justification ever since?

🧠 Check Your Understanding
1. Every token produces three vectors: Q, K, and V. Explain what each one represents — and crucially, why Key and Value are two separate things rather than one.
Q (Query) = the question a token broadcasts to the whole sequence: "What information am I looking for right now?" This is the token acting as a seeker. K (Key) = the advertisement a token puts on itself: "Here is what I contain — what I can offer to anyone searching." This controls whether the token gets attended to. V (Value) = the actual content the token delivers once it has been selected: "This is the information you get from me." This is the payload. Why separate K and V? Because a token may need to advertise itself differently for different relationship types (Key), while delivering different content once selected (Value). The Key controls searchability — whether you get found. The Value controls content — what you contribute once found. Separating them lets the model learn both independently. Concretely: the word "bank" in "river bank" needs a Key that says "geographic feature" so geographic queries find it, and a Value that encodes riparian content. In "money at the bank," the Key says "financial institution" and the Value encodes financial content. Same word, different Keys, different Values — learned through separate weight matrices W_K and W_V. If K and V were the same, the model couldn't control its searchability independently from its content.
2. Trace the full attention computation for "it" in "The cat sat on the mat because it was comfortable." What does each step do?
Step 1 — Projection: "it" produces Q by multiplying its embedding through W_Q. This Query encodes something like "I am a pronoun looking for my antecedent noun." Every other word simultaneously produces its Key (W_K) and Value (W_V). Step 2 — Scoring: "it"'s Query is dotted against every word's Key. "Cat"'s Key says "animate noun, subject." "Mat"'s Key says "inanimate noun, location." Both score well against a pronoun-seeking query. "Sat" (verb), "because" (conjunction), "was" (auxiliary) score lower. Step 3 — Scale and softmax: scores divided by √d_k, then softmax converts to a probability distribution. "Mat" gets weight ~0.55 (closest antecedent, inanimate location matching "comfortable"). "Cat" gets ~0.25 (animate, further away). Other words share the remaining ~0.20. Step 4 — Value aggregation: "it" computes a weighted sum of every word's Value. 55% of "mat"'s Value + 25% of "cat"'s Value + smaller contributions from other words. "it"'s new representation now encodes: the pronoun "it" refers primarily to "mat" in this specific context — the meaning has been resolved. This context-resolution happened in one pass, with no sequential processing required.
3. Why is the attention formula divided by √d_k? What goes wrong if you remove the scaling?
Two random d_k-dimensional vectors with zero mean and unit variance produce a dot product with variance d_k — standard deviation √d_k. For d_k=64, σ≈8; for d_k=512, σ≈22. Without scaling, one score in the softmax is typically much larger than the others. Softmax exponentiation amplifies differences: if score A is 22 and score B is 20, exp(22)/exp(20) ≈ 7.4 — A gets most of the probability. In the extreme, softmax assigns ≈1 to the maximum score and ≈0 to everything else. In this saturated regime, the derivative of softmax is nearly zero everywhere. Backpropagating through near-zero softmax gradients produces near-zero updates to W_Q and W_K — the attention weights can't change, and the model stops learning how to attend. The failure mode is insidious: early training looks fine because randomly-initialised weights are small. As Q and K grow during training, scores grow too, softmax saturates increasingly, gradients shrink, and training either stalls or diverges. The effect is more severe at larger d_k — which is exactly the scale used in BERT and GPT. Dividing by √d_k brings the dot product variance back to ≈1 regardless of d_k, keeping softmax in its gradient-rich middle range throughout training.
4. What is multi-head attention and why does it work better than single-head at full dimension?
Multi-head attention runs H independent attention operations in parallel, each with its own W_Q^h, W_K^h, W_V^h matrices, each operating in d_k = d_model/H dimensions. A single head computes one score per token pair — one type of relationship. Language has many simultaneous relationship types: syntactic (subject-verb agreement), semantic (synonymy), coreference (pronoun-antecedent), positional (nearby context), long-range discourse. A single head must average over all of these in one probability distribution, which means it can't specialise deeply in any one type. Multiple heads each specialise: mechanistic interpretability research (Anthropic, DeepMind) consistently finds that specific heads track pronoun resolution, subject-verb agreement, bracket matching in code, sentence boundary detection. This specialisation emerges from training — it's not programmed. Total compute is approximately the same as single-head at full dimension (H × d_model/H = d_model per layer), but representational richness is H times greater because each head can attend to orthogonal aspects simultaneously. The W_O projection after concatenation learns to optimally combine the H heads' outputs into a single coherent representation.
5. Why is the KV-cache important for generation, and why is Q not cached?
During autoregressive generation, the model produces one token at a time. Without caching, generating token n requires computing K and V for all n-1 previous tokens from scratch at every step — total cost O(n²) for generating n tokens. The KV-cache stores K and V vectors once they're computed. At step n, only the new token's K and V need to be computed; all previous K/V are retrieved from cache. Total cost drops to O(n) — linear instead of quadratic. For 128k-context generation, this is the difference between feasible and impossible inference latency. Q is not cached because of what Q, K, and V represent. K = "what each context token can offer" — this is a fixed property of a token in context. Whether token 5 is "cat" or "Paris" doesn't change once it's in the sequence; its Key stays the same at every generation step. V = "what each context token delivers" — same reasoning; fixed. Q = "what the current generation step is looking for" — this is fundamentally different at each step. When generating token 100, Q encodes what token 100 is searching for among tokens 1–99. When generating token 101, Q encodes what token 101 is looking for. Q changes every step because the question changes every step. The cache is valid precisely because K and V are properties of the context, not of the current step.
⚡ Quick Recap — Attention & the Transformer
  1. The core idea: every word looks at every other word simultaneously. Relationships are computed directly between any pair — no sequential processing, no distance penalty.
  2. Q — Query: "What am I looking for?" Each token broadcasts a question to the sequence. Produced by multiplying the token embedding through learned matrix W_Q.
  3. K — Key: "What can I offer? How should I be found?" Each token's searchable label — controls whether it gets selected. Produced through W_K. Separate from Value because searchability should be independent of content.
  4. V — Value: "What is my actual content?" The payload delivered once a token is selected. Produced through W_V. The Key gets you found; the Value is what you give.
  5. The K/V separation insight: the same word in different contexts needs different Keys (advertise differently) and different Values (deliver different content). Learned independently through separate W_K and W_V matrices.
  6. Four computation steps: (1) Scores = Q·Kᵀ — relevance of every pair; (2) Scale by 1/√d_k — prevent softmax saturation and vanishing gradients; (3) Softmax → attention weights (prob. distribution per token); (4) Weighted sum of Values → new contextual representation.
  7. Multi-head attention: H heads, each with own W_Q/K/V, each specialising in a different relationship type. Same compute as single-head; H times richer representation. Outputs mixed through W_O.
  8. Causal masking: add −∞ to upper-triangle positions before softmax → future tokens get weight 0. Enables GPT-style autoregressive generation.
  9. Transformer block: Multi-Head Attention + FFN, each wrapped with residual connections (gradient highway) and LayerNorm (stable activations). Attention mixes across positions; FFN transforms within each position.
  10. KV-cache: K and V are fixed properties of context tokens — cache them once. Q changes every generation step — don't cache. Reduces generation cost from O(n²) to O(n).
🎯 Interview Questions
  1. What is self-attention? What problem does it solve compared to RNNs?
    Self-attention computes relationships between every token pair in a sequence simultaneously. Each token produces Q, K, V; Q is dotted against every K for relevance scores; softmax gives attention weights; output is a weighted sum of V vectors. Solves the long-range dependency problem: RNNs require information to travel through every intermediate hidden state — degrading over distance. In attention, word 2 and word 50 have a direct connection via Q·K dot product. Distance is irrelevant.
  2. What are Q, K, and V? Why are Key and Value separate projections?
    Q = what this token is looking for. K = how this token advertises itself to be found. V = what this token actually delivers once selected. They're separate because a token may need to control its searchability (Key) independently from its content (Value). "Bank" in "river bank" needs a geographic Key but delivers riparian content. In "money bank" it needs a financial Key but delivers financial content. Separate W_K and W_V matrices let the model learn these independently. If they were the same, a token couldn't adjust its searchability without changing its content.
  3. Why does the formula divide by √d_k?
    Two d_k-dimensional random vectors have a dot product with standard deviation √d_k. Without scaling, scores are too large, pushing softmax toward saturation (one score gets weight ≈1, all others ≈0). In this regime, the gradient of softmax is ≈0 — W_Q and W_K stop receiving meaningful gradient signal and training stalls. Dividing by √d_k keeps dot products at ≈unit variance regardless of dimension, maintaining softmax in its gradient-rich regime throughout training.
  4. What is multi-head attention and what do different heads learn?
    H independent attention operations in parallel, each with own W_Q/K/V, each in d_k=d_model/H subspace. Each head can specialise in a different relationship type. Research finds consistent specialisation: some heads track subject-verb agreement, some coreference, some bracket matching in code, some local context. Same total compute as single-head at full dimension, but H times richer — each head attends to orthogonal aspects. W_O after concatenation learns to optimally combine all heads.
  5. What is the KV-cache and why is Q not cached?
    The KV-cache stores K and V vectors for all context tokens, avoiding recomputation at each generation step. Without it, generating n tokens costs O(n²) total (recompute all K/V at each step); with it, O(n) (one new K/V per step, rest from cache). K and V are cached because they represent fixed properties of context tokens — what a token offers and delivers doesn't change once it's in the sequence. Q is not cached because it represents what the current generation step is looking for — this changes at every step as the model produces a new token.
