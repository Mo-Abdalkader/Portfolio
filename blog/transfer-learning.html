Transfer Learning & Fine-Tuning:
Standing on the Shoulders of Millions of Images

You don't need a million labelled brain MRIs to build a brain tumour classifier. You need a model that already understands images — and a few hundred examples of your specific task. This is transfer learning, and it changes everything about practical deep learning.

← Back to Blog ☰ Categories

A newly graduated radiologist doesn't start their first day knowing nothing.
They bring twelve years of education, thousands of hours of anatomy,
an entire medical school's worth of pattern recognition.
On their first day, they apply all of that knowledge to the specific cases in front of them.

A pre-trained neural network is that radiologist on day one.
It already knows how to see. You just need to show it what to look for.

01 — The Core Idea: Don't Start from Zero

Training a powerful neural network from scratch requires enormous amounts of labelled data. Most real-world problems don't have that luxury. Medical imaging datasets are expensive to annotate — expert radiologists must label each scan. Legal document classifiers need expert review. Industrial defect detectors rely on rare failure examples. The most valuable tasks are often exactly where data is scarcest.

Transfer learning is the solution. Instead of initialising weights randomly and training entirely on your small dataset, you start from a model already trained on a large, related dataset — and transfer its learned knowledge to your task. You keep what it already knows, then adapt it to what you need.

💡 Why It Works

Any deep neural network learns in layers of abstraction. Early layers capture low-level patterns — edges and textures in a vision model, grammar and syntax in a language model. These low-level patterns are universal: they appear across domains because edges exist in every image, and grammar exists in every text, regardless of subject matter.

Transfer learning exploits this: borrow the general-purpose layers, replace only the task-specific output head. The model already knows how to perceive — you just teach it what to look for in your domain.

This isn't a computer vision trick. It's the operating principle behind the entire modern AI landscape. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — all pre-trained foundation models fine-tuned for specific tasks. The CNN fine-tuning we'll use as an example today and the LoRA fine-tuning of LLMs in Day 25 are the same idea at different scales.

02 — What Fine-Tuning Actually Means

Fine-tuning is taking a pre-trained model and continuing to train it — partially or fully — on a new, smaller dataset. The key is that not all layers need the same treatment. Layers that learned general knowledge should barely change. Layers that learned task-specific patterns need more adaptation. The output head, trained for the original task's classes, must be replaced entirely.

Early layers
General knowledge — almost never retrain. In CNNs: edge and texture detectors. In LLMs: grammar and basic syntax. In audio models: frequency patterns. These are universal across domains and architectures. Retraining them wastes compute and risks losing the foundations everything else is built on.
Middle layers
Intermediate patterns — fine-tune cautiously. In CNNs: shapes and structures. In LLMs: semantic relationships and reasoning. These begin to encode domain knowledge, so gentle adaptation with a very small learning rate helps without destroying the foundation.
Deep layers
Task-specific — fine-tune more aggressively. In CNNs: object-specific detectors. In LLMs: style, domain vocabulary, instruction-following. These are most tightly coupled to the original task and need the most adaptation for a new domain.
Output head
Always replace. The original head predicts the original task's outputs — ImageNet's 1,000 categories, next-token probabilities, etc. For any new task this layer is useless and must be swapped for a fresh one matching your output space.
🔬 Why Early Layers Are Universal

Yosinski et al. (2014) measured this experimentally. The first layers of any neural network trained on natural data learn nearly identical features regardless of the specific task. CNNs trained on ImageNet, Places, or medical images all develop similar edge and texture detectors in layer 1. The generality of early representations is a fundamental property of how gradient descent shapes deep networks — not an accident of architecture. You can rely on it.

03 — Feature Extraction vs Fine-Tuning

Transfer learning has two operating modes. The right choice depends on how different your task is from the pre-training task, and how much data you have.

DimensionFeature ExtractionFine-Tuning
What trainsNew output head onlyHead + some or all backbone
Pre-trained weightsCompletely frozenUnfrozen, updated at tiny LR
Data needed✓ Works with very little~ Needs more data
Speed✓ Fast — no backbone gradients✗ Slower — full backprop
Risk✓ Zero — weights untouched✗ Catastrophic forgetting if LR too large
Accuracy ceiling~ Good✓ Best possible
Best forSimilar domain, tiny datasetDifferent domain, more data, max accuracy

In practice, do both — sequentially. Start frozen (head only) for 5–10 epochs until the head converges. Then unfreeze and fine-tune at a much smaller learning rate. Skipping Phase 1 is dangerous: a randomly-initialised head produces large gradients that flow backward and overwrite the pre-trained backbone in the very first updates.

⚠️ The Learning Rate Rule

When you unfreeze for fine-tuning, the learning rate must drop dramatically. Pre-trained weights only need tiny nudges — they're already near a good solution. Using the same rate as head training overwrites them in just a few steps.

Rule of thumb: fine-tuning LR = head LR ÷ 10 to ÷ 100. When in doubt, go smaller — slow convergence is recoverable, destroyed weights are not.

04 — Data Augmentation: The Other Lever

Transfer learning solves the "not enough data to train deep features" problem. Augmentation solves the second problem: not enough data to prevent the new head from memorising your small training set.

Augmentation teaches invariances — the label shouldn't change when you rotate, flip, or adjust brightness. You're enforcing: "a glioma is still a glioma at 15 degrees." Each augmented view isn't new ground truth — it's a different perspective on the same example. Over many epochs with a few stochastic transforms, a 3,000-image dataset can produce ~360,000 distinct training views: a 400% effective expansion at zero labelling cost.

One rule is absolute: augmentation is for training only. Validation transforms must be deterministic. Augmenting validation makes accuracy non-deterministic across runs, which makes overfitting undetectable.

05 — The General Workflow

1
Choose a pre-trained model close to your domain. For images: ResNet50, EfficientNet, ViT. For text: BERT, RoBERTa, GPT-2. For audio: Wav2Vec. Domain-specific models (CheXNet for radiology, BioBERT for biomedical text) transfer even better when the target domain differs from general photographs or Wikipedia text.
2
Replace the output head. Discard the original output layer — ImageNet's 1,000-class softmax, next-token prediction, etc. — and attach a fresh head matching your task. Classification, regression, sequence labelling — whatever your output space requires. Only this head starts with random weights.
3
Freeze backbone, train head first. Disable gradients for all backbone parameters. Train only the new head for 5–10 epochs at a normal learning rate. This lets the head stabilise before any gradients touch the pre-trained weights.
4
Unfreeze and fine-tune at a tiny LR. Enable gradients on the backbone and continue training at 1/10–1/100 of the head learning rate. The backbone now nudges its representations toward your domain. A learning rate scheduler (cosine annealing) decays LR further over time for stable convergence.
5
Evaluate properly — not just accuracy. Measure per-class metrics, especially with imbalanced data. A model predicting the majority class everywhere can look accurate while being useless. For medical tasks, sensitivity and specificity matter more than overall accuracy.

06 — CNN Example: Brain Tumour Classifier at 95%

Here's the workflow applied to a concrete case — the brain tumour project from yesterday's CNN post. The dataset has 3,000 MRI scans (glioma, meningioma, pituitary, no tumour). Too few to train a deep CNN from scratch without severe overfitting. A plain CNN trained from random weights achieves ~63%. With a pre-trained ResNet50 and the two-phase strategy, it reaches 95.5%. The 32-point gap is entirely borrowed knowledge.

import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torchvision
from torchvision import transforms, datasets
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
import numpy as np

# ── Hyperparameters ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
NUM_CLASSES = 4          # glioma, meningioma, pituitary, no_tumor
IMG_SIZE    = 224
BATCH_SIZE  = 32
HEAD_LR     = 1e-3       # Phase 1: head-only training
FINETUNE_LR = 1e-5       # Phase 2: full fine-tuning
HEAD_EPOCHS = 8          # Phase 1 epochs
FT_EPOCHS   = 22         # Phase 2 epochs
DEVICE      = 'cuda' if torch.cuda.is_available() else 'cpu'

# ── Data Transforms ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
train_tfm = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.Resize(256),
    transforms.RandomCrop(IMG_SIZE),
    transforms.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    transforms.RandomVerticalFlip(p=0.3),
    transforms.RandomRotation(20),
    transforms.ColorJitter(brightness=0.4, contrast=0.4),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize([0.485, 0.456, 0.406], [0.229, 0.224, 0.225])
])
val_tfm = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.Resize(256), transforms.CenterCrop(IMG_SIZE),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    transforms.Normalize([0.485, 0.456, 0.406], [0.229, 0.224, 0.225])
])

# ── Data Loaders ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
# Dataset structure: data/train/{glioma,meningioma,pituitary,no_tumor}/
train_ds = datasets.ImageFolder('data/train', transform=train_tfm)
val_ds   = datasets.ImageFolder('data/val',   transform=val_tfm)
train_dl = DataLoader(train_ds, batch_size=BATCH_SIZE, shuffle=True,  num_workers=4)
val_dl   = DataLoader(val_ds,   batch_size=BATCH_SIZE, shuffle=False, num_workers=4)
print(f"Train: {len(train_ds)} | Val: {len(val_ds)}")
print(f"Classes: {train_ds.classes}")

# ── Model Definition ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
class TumourClassifier(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_classes=NUM_CLASSES):
        super().__init__()
        # Load ResNet50 with ImageNet weights — NEVER use random init for backbone
        self.backbone = torchvision.models.resnet50(weights='IMAGENET1K_V2')
        in_features = self.backbone.fc.in_features  # 2048 for ResNet50

        # Replace final layer with task-specific head
        self.backbone.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(in_features, 256),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(0.4),
            nn.Linear(256, num_classes)
        )

    def freeze_backbone(self):
        """Phase 1: freeze everything except the new head."""
        for name, param in self.backbone.named_parameters():
            if 'fc' not in name:  # 'fc' is our new head
                param.requires_grad = False

    def unfreeze_backbone(self):
        """Phase 2: enable gradients for all layers."""
        for param in self.backbone.parameters():
            param.requires_grad = True

    def trainable_params(self):
        return sum(p.numel() for p in self.parameters() if p.requires_grad)

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.backbone(x)

# ── Training Helper ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
def run_epoch(model, loader, criterion, optimizer=None):
    training = optimizer is not None
    model.train() if training else model.eval()
    total_loss, correct = 0.0, 0
    ctx = torch.enable_grad() if training else torch.no_grad()
    with ctx:
        for imgs, labels in loader:
            imgs, labels = imgs.to(DEVICE), labels.to(DEVICE)
            logits = model(imgs)
            loss = criterion(logits, labels)
            if training:
                optimizer.zero_grad(); loss.backward(); optimizer.step()
            total_loss += loss.item() * imgs.size(0)
            correct += (logits.argmax(1) == labels).sum().item()
    n = len(loader.dataset)
    return total_loss / n, correct / n

# ── Main Training Loop ───────────────────────────────────────────────
model = TumourClassifier().to(DEVICE)
criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()

# ─── PHASE 1: Head-only training (frozen backbone) ───────────────────
print("\n=== Phase 1: Head Training (frozen backbone) ===")
model.freeze_backbone()
print(f"Trainable params: {model.trainable_params():,}")
optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(
    filter(lambda p: p.requires_grad, model.parameters()), lr=HEAD_LR
)
for epoch in range(1, HEAD_EPOCHS + 1):
    tr_loss, tr_acc = run_epoch(model, train_dl, criterion, optimizer)
    va_loss, va_acc = run_epoch(model, val_dl, criterion)
    if epoch % 2 == 0:
        print(f"  ep {epoch:02d} | tr_acc={tr_acc:.4f} | va_acc={va_acc:.4f} | va_loss={va_loss:.4f}")

# ─── PHASE 2: Fine-tuning (unfrozen backbone, tiny lr) ───────────────
print("\n=== Phase 2: Fine-Tuning (unfrozen backbone) ===")
model.unfreeze_backbone()
print(f"Trainable params: {model.trainable_params():,}")
optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=FINETUNE_LR, weight_decay=1e-4)
scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=FT_EPOCHS)
best_acc, best_state = 0.0, None
for epoch in range(1, FT_EPOCHS + 1):
    tr_loss, tr_acc = run_epoch(model, train_dl, criterion, optimizer)
    va_loss, va_acc = run_epoch(model, val_dl, criterion)
    scheduler.step()
    if va_acc > best_acc:
        best_acc = va_acc
        best_state = {k: v.cpu().clone() for k, v in model.state_dict().items()}
    if epoch % 5 == 0:
        print(f"  ep {epoch:02d} | tr_acc={tr_acc:.4f} | va_acc={va_acc:.4f} | best={best_acc:.4f}")

print(f"\nBest val accuracy: {best_acc:.4f}")

📊 Reading the Training Curve

Notice the Phase 1 progression: val accuracy jumps from 74% to 89% in just 8 epochs — even though the backbone is completely frozen. This is the power of borrowed representations. The head is simply learning a linear decision boundary in a rich 2048-dimensional feature space that ImageNet pre-training already organised well. Then Phase 2 fine-tuning pushes it from 89% to 95.5% by allowing the backbone to shift its representations toward brain MRI–specific features. The two-phase training is 6× faster than training from scratch and achieves 32 percentage points higher accuracy.

07 — The Decision Framework: Which Approach for Which Situation

Transfer learning isn't one-size-fits-all. The right approach depends on two axes: how similar your target domain is to the pre-training domain, and how much labelled data you have. Every combination has a recommended strategy.

Similar domain · Small data (<5K)
Feature extraction only. The pre-trained features work well as-is. Train only the head. Frozen backbone prevents overfitting on the tiny dataset. Example: classifying plant species from smartphone photos using ImageNet-pretrained backbone.
Similar domain · Large data (>10K)
Feature extraction then fine-tune. Start with frozen backbone for head convergence, then fine-tune everything. More data means less overfitting risk during fine-tuning. Example: large product image catalogue classification.
Different domain · Small data (<5K)
Feature extraction with cautious fine-tuning of top layers only. The early ImageNet features are still useful (edges, textures), but deep layers need more domain-specific adaptation. Unfreeze only the last 1–2 convolutional blocks. Example: histopathology slide classification, satellite imagery analysis.
Different domain · Large data (>10K)
Full fine-tuning, or domain-specific pre-training. If you have enough data, full fine-tuning can adapt even early layers. Better: use a backbone pre-trained on domain-similar data (RadImageNet for radiology, CheXNet for chest X-rays, BiomedCLIP for biomedical). Example: large hospital MRI database, national radiology archive.

08 — The Same Idea Powers the Entire LLM Ecosystem

Transfer learning for CNNs is not a historical curiosity — it is the foundational concept underlying modern language AI, and understanding the vision version gives you deep intuition for why language fine-tuning works.

🔗 CNN Transfer Learning → LLM Fine-Tuning

When OpenAI fine-tunes GPT-4 on medical Q&A, or Anthropic trains Claude on human preference data, the same two-phase logic applies: pre-train on massive general data → adapt to specific task. The "frozen backbone" in LLMs corresponds to keeping most model weights frozen and only training a small adapter. The "new head" corresponds to the LoRA low-rank matrices added to specific weight matrices.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), covered in Day 25, is essentially the vision "freeze backbone, train head" idea taken to an extreme: rather than training any full layers, it adds tiny rank-decomposed matrices (A and B) to existing weight matrices. The number of trainable parameters drops from billions to millions — the same efficiency principle as training only the 525K-parameter head instead of the full 24M-parameter ResNet.

Even RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) fits the pattern: a base LLM (the pre-trained backbone) is fine-tuned on human preference signals (the domain-specific dataset), with careful learning rate management to avoid catastrophic forgetting of general language capabilities. Transfer learning for CNNs is not a separate topic from LLMs — it's the same idea at a different scale.

💬 For You to Consider

The brain tumour classifier reached 95% accuracy using transfer learning from a model trained on everyday photographs — dogs, cars, landscapes. At some level, the visual features that distinguish a glioma from a meningioma were latent in a model that had never seen a brain scan. That's a remarkable and slightly unsettling fact about the universality of learned representations.

The model was never told: "look for irregular boundaries" or "focus on the brightness distribution in the tumour core." It discovered that glioma boundaries look a certain way by gradient descent alone — guided by the visual primitives that ImageNet had already taught it to detect.

If the same visual representations that recognise cat ears also help detect tumours, what does that suggest about the nature of visual intelligence? And should we trust a medical model whose "prior knowledge" came from Instagram-style photographs?

🧠 Check Your Understanding
1. What is the core problem transfer learning solves — and why does it work across completely different domains like photos and medical scans?
Transfer learning solves the data scarcity problem: training deep models from scratch requires far more labelled data than most real-world tasks can provide. It works across domains because neural networks learn in layers of abstraction. Early layers always learn low-level patterns — edges, textures, basic structures — that are universal across any domain using similar input types. A CNN trained on cat photos learns edge detectors in layer 1 that are essentially identical to the edge detectors in a CNN trained on medical scans. Since these early representations are domain-agnostic, they transfer directly. Only the deeper, task-specific layers need adaptation. You borrow the universal foundations and adapt only the specialised top.
2. What is the difference between feature extraction and fine-tuning, and why should you always do feature extraction first before fine-tuning?
Feature extraction freezes all pre-trained weights and trains only the new output head — the backbone is a fixed feature converter. Fine-tuning unfreezes the backbone and continues training with a very small learning rate, letting representations adapt to the new domain. You must always do feature extraction first because the new head starts with random weights, producing large chaotic gradients at the output. If those gradients flow into the pre-trained backbone before the head has stabilised, they overwrite carefully accumulated weights in just a few steps — catastrophic forgetting. Running 5–10 epochs of head-only training first brings the head to stable weights with small, well-directed gradients. Only then is it safe to unfreeze the backbone for fine-tuning.
3. Why must the fine-tuning learning rate be 10–100× smaller than the head training learning rate — and what happens if you use the same rate for both?
Pre-trained weights encode millions of gradient steps of accumulated information. They are already near a good solution for general feature detection — they only need small nudges toward the new domain, not large updates. A learning rate of 1e-3 was appropriate for training the head from random initialisation (large random error → large needed correction). Applying the same rate to pre-trained backbone weights makes updates ~100× too large, overwriting the learned representations in just a few gradient steps. This is catastrophic forgetting — the model forgets its ImageNet knowledge and tries to relearn everything from your small dataset, which it can't do well. A rate of 1e-5 makes tiny adjustments: the backbone adapts gradually while preserving its general-purpose feature vocabulary.
4. In the brain tumour CNN example, Phase 1 (frozen backbone, 8 epochs) already reaches ~89% accuracy. Why does the frozen backbone already produce such strong results before any fine-tuning?
A ResNet50 backbone pre-trained on ImageNet converts each image into a 2048-dimensional feature vector. After millions of gradient steps on 1.2M images, this vector space is already richly organised — visually similar images cluster together, visual dissimilarities produce distance in the embedding space. Training the head is not training a vision model; it's learning a linear decision boundary in this already well-structured space. Because ImageNet's general visual features (textures, shapes, structural patterns) partially overlap with the features that distinguish tumour types (boundary sharpness, tissue texture, structural asymmetry), the pre-trained features are already meaningful for the task — even without any adaptation. Phase 2 fine-tuning then pushes that 89% to 95% by allowing the deep backbone layers to shift from "general visual object detector" to "brain MRI pattern detector."
5. How is LoRA for LLMs the same concept as "freeze backbone, train head" — and why does this matter for understanding modern AI fine-tuning?
Both strategies are built on the same insight: most of a pre-trained model's weights encode general knowledge that doesn't need to change for a new task. "Freeze backbone, train head" freezes the entire backbone and trains a small output layer — so only ~525K of 24M parameters are trainable. LoRA does this more surgically: instead of freezing everything and training a separate head, it freezes all original weight matrices and adds tiny rank-decomposed matrices (A × B, where rank is 8–64) alongside specific layers. Only A and B train — dropping trainable parameters from billions to millions with minimal accuracy loss. Both methods share the core idea: borrow general representations, adapt a small targeted part, avoid updating weights that are already good. Understanding CNN transfer learning gives you the deep intuition for why LoRA works — the efficiency comes from the same source: most of a pre-trained model's knowledge is general and reusable.
⚡ Quick Recap — Transfer Learning & Fine-Tuning
  1. The core idea: Don't start from zero. Borrow a model pre-trained on a large dataset, keep its learned representations, replace only the output head, and adapt to your task with a fraction of the data and compute.
  2. Why it works: Early layers always learn general, universal patterns — edges in vision, grammar in language. These transfer across domains. Only deep, task-specific layers need adaptation.
  3. Feature extraction = frozen backbone, train new head only. Fast, safe, works with very little data. The backbone is a fixed feature converter.
  4. Fine-tuning = unfreeze backbone, continue training at a tiny learning rate. Better accuracy, needs more data, catastrophic forgetting risk if LR is too large.
  5. Always do both sequentially: freeze → train head (5–10 epochs) → unfreeze → fine-tune at 1/10–1/100 of head LR. Skipping Phase 1 destroys pre-trained weights on the first updates.
  6. The learning rate rule: fine-tuning LR = head LR ÷ 10 to ÷ 100. At 1e-3 for the head, fine-tune the backbone at 1e-4 to 1e-5. Slow convergence is recoverable; destroyed weights are not.
  7. Augmentation is the primary regulariser for small datasets — teaches invariances and gives 4–5× effective data expansion. Training only, never validation.
  8. CNN example: 3,000 brain MRIs, ResNet50 backbone → 95.5% accuracy vs. 63% from scratch. The 32-point gap is entirely borrowed knowledge from ImageNet photographs.
  9. Domain-specific pre-training (RadImageNet, BioBERT, CheXNet) beats ImageNet when your target domain diverges significantly from natural photos or Wikipedia text.
  10. The same concept powers LLMs: LoRA freezes most weights and trains tiny rank-decomposed adapters; RLHF fine-tunes on human preferences. Transfer learning is the unifying idea behind all of modern AI.
🎯 Interview Questions
  1. What is transfer learning and why does it work?
    Transfer learning reuses weights from a model pre-trained on a large dataset as the starting point for a new, smaller task. It works because neural networks learn in layers of abstraction — early layers capture universal patterns (edges, grammar) that appear across domains. These transfer directly; only the deeper, task-specific layers need adaptation. It's most useful when your target dataset is small and the source and target domains share low-level structure.
  2. What is the difference between feature extraction and fine-tuning?
    Feature extraction freezes all pre-trained weights and trains only a new output head — the backbone is a fixed feature converter. Fine-tuning unfreezes the backbone and continues training with a very small learning rate, allowing representations to adapt to the new domain. Feature extraction is faster and needs less data; fine-tuning achieves better accuracy when data allows. Best practice: do both sequentially — freeze first, then fine-tune.
  3. Why use a very small learning rate when fine-tuning?
    Pre-trained weights encode millions of gradient steps of useful information. A large learning rate overwrites them in just a few updates — catastrophic forgetting. A tiny rate (1e-5) makes small nudges: the backbone adapts gradually toward the new domain while preserving the general-purpose knowledge that made transfer learning valuable in the first place.
  4. What is catastrophic forgetting and how do you prevent it?
    Catastrophic forgetting occurs when backpropagation with a large learning rate overwrites pre-trained weights, destroying accumulated knowledge. Prevented with two-phase training: Phase 1 freezes the backbone so no destructive gradients reach it while the randomly-initialised head stabilises. Phase 2 unfreezes at 1e-5 so each update is a tiny adjustment rather than an overwrite.
  5. How does CNN transfer learning relate to LoRA for LLMs?
    Both exploit the same insight: most of a pre-trained model's weights encode general knowledge that doesn't need to change. "Freeze backbone, train head" does this literally. LoRA does it more surgically — instead of a full output layer, it adds tiny rank-decomposed matrices (A × B) alongside frozen weight matrices. Only A and B train, cutting trainable parameters from billions to millions. Same principle, different implementation, same source of efficiency.
